Stages of learning — Fitts & Posner (1967)

Fitts and Posner describe skill acquisition across three stages, each with distinct characteristics in attention demand, movement quality, and error detection ability.

StageAlso calledKey characteristicsAttention demand
CognitiveVerbal-motorLarge performance gains; many errors; inconsistent; relies on verbal instructions and demonstrations; thinks about each movement stepVery high — focus on what to do
AssociativeMotor stageFewer errors; movements smoother; self-evaluation begins; gradual, smaller improvements over timeModerate — refining how to do it
AutonomousAutomatic stageHighly consistent; movement is automatic; can detect and correct own errors; attention freed for tactics and environmentLow — skill runs on autopilot
Exam tip: Always link the stage of learning to the appropriate practice type, feedback type, and coaching strategy. Cognitive learners need frequent, specific KP; autonomous learners benefit from bandwidth/summary feedback and random practice.
Bernstein's model of motor learning

Bernstein proposed that learners manage complexity by controlling degrees of freedom (the independent components of the movement system).

PhaseWhat happens
FreezeNovice restricts joint movement to simplify control — movement appears stiff and robotic
ReleaseAs skill improves, degrees of freedom are progressively unlocked — movement becomes more fluid
ExploitExpert uses reactive/passive forces (momentum, gravity) for economical, efficient movement
Skill classification continua

Skills are not classified as purely one type — they sit on continua. Use precise language: "this skill tends toward the open end of the open–closed continuum because…"

ContinuumOne endOther endSport examples
Muscular involvementGross (large muscle groups)Fine (small, precise muscles)Sprinting (gross); dart throw (fine)
Environmental contextOpen (unpredictable environment)Closed (stable, predictable)Netball pass (open); gymnastics vault (closed)
DiscretenessDiscrete (clear start/end)Continuous (no clear start/end)Penalty kick (disc); cycling (cont); triple jump (serial)
PacingExternally pacedSelf-pacedBatting in cricket (ext); golf drive (self)
Exam tip: When classifying a skill, name the continuum, identify where the skill sits on it, AND justify with a specific feature of the skill or its environment. Avoid one-word answers.
Why classification matters for coaching
Skill classification directly informs practice design. Open, externally paced skills require variable practice in representative, unpredictable environments to build adaptability. Closed, self-paced skills benefit from blocked, repetitive practice to build automaticity and a strong motor programme. Gross skills with complex serial structure may suit part-practice; continuous skills suit whole-practice to preserve rhythm and timing.

Feedback & stage of learning

The type and frequency of feedback should be matched to the learner's stage:

  • Cognitive stage: Frequent, specific KP — helps build accurate mental reference of correct movement; concurrent and terminal feedback both appropriate
  • Associative stage: Reduce frequency; encourage self-evaluation; introduce summary feedback; specific KP still valuable for refining errors
  • Autonomous stage: Bandwidth or summary feedback — promotes self-reliance; over-provision of augmented feedback can cause dependency and impair intrinsic processing
Exam tip: Explain why a feedback type is appropriate — link frequency and type to the learner's ability to detect and correct their own errors. Never just name a feedback type without justification.
